# Worked Example: A Nonprofit Considering a Paid Service

This fictional example shows Standard mode. It demonstrates the artifacts and concise rationales an orchestrator should expose; it does not reveal private chain-of-thought.

## User question

> Our community nonprofit provides free employment workshops. Donations are unstable. Should we launch a paid training service for employers, and how might that affect our mission?

## Shared Brief

**Core question:** Which viable models could add dependable earned income without weakening free community access or staff capacity?

**Exploration goal:** Reveal materially different service and funding models, their mission effects, operating requirements, and the evidence needed before commitment.

**Scope:** Employer-facing training launched within twelve months using the current nonprofit entity and staff base.

**Exclusions:** Forming a separate commercial company, national expansion, or replacing free workshops.

**Stakeholders:** Job seekers, nonprofit staff, donors, employer customers, community partners, and the board.

**Constraints:** [FACT] Free workshops must continue. [FACT] The team has six full-time staff. [ASSUMPTION] At least some employers will pay for customized training. [UNKNOWN] Staff delivery capacity and willingness to pay have not been tested.

**Exploration criteria:** Mission protection, income reliability, delivery feasibility, reversibility, staff sustainability, and learning value.

## Council and coverage

| Role | Unique mandate |
|---|---|
| Constructive explorer | Develop distinct earned-income mechanisms and combinations |
| Critical challenger | Test mission drift, cross-subsidy claims, and operational failure |
| Evidence and assumptions auditor | Classify claims and define validation needs |
| Beneficiary advocate | Protect free access, dignity, and participant outcomes |
| Frontline operator | Test staffing, scheduling, service quality, and recovery from overload |

## Blind first-round summaries

### Constructive explorer

**Thesis:** The choice is not “commercialize or do nothing”; the nonprofit can separate payer, beneficiary, and delivery models.

**Possibilities:** (1) bespoke employer workshops, (2) a standardized cohort course, (3) employer sponsorship of community seats, and (4) licensed curriculum delivered by partners.

**Strongest proposal:** Start with a narrow standardized pilot plus a community-seat sponsorship component. [INFERENCE] Standardization offers more learning per unit of staff effort than bespoke consulting.

**Disconfirming signal:** Employers demand extensive customization or show low repeat intent.

### Critical challenger

**Thesis:** Earned income can destabilize the mission if paying clients gain priority over beneficiaries before governance rules exist.

**Material risks:** Staff attention shifts to employer deadlines, free workshops receive less preparation, and revenue is mistaken for unrestricted surplus despite selling and delivery costs.

**Strongest proposal:** Do not launch until the board defines a mission floor, capacity ceiling, cost-allocation method, and stop condition.

**Disconfirming signal:** A ring-fenced pilot demonstrates that free-program quality and staff workload remain stable.

### Evidence and assumptions auditor

**Thesis:** The strongest claims are currently assumptions: willingness to pay, usable staff capacity, repeat demand, and positive unit economics.

- [FACT] The nonprofit has six full-time staff.
- [ASSUMPTION] Employer buyers value the nonprofit's expertise enough to pay.
- [ASSUMPTION] Existing staff can deliver paid work without reducing free-program quality.
- [INFERENCE] A standardized offer will have better margins than custom work.
- [VALUE] Free community access should have priority over revenue growth.
- [UNKNOWN] Acquisition cost, delivery cost, price tolerance, repeat rate, and staff interest.

**Strongest proposal:** Run paid discovery before building a full service: ten buyer interviews, three letters of intent, and one priced pilot.

### Beneficiary advocate

**Thesis:** A paid service is defensible only when beneficiaries receive a visible benefit and cannot be displaced by customers with more power.

**Strongest proposal:** Tie the service to a published community benefit: each employer cohort funds a defined number of free workshop places or coaching hours.

**Risk:** Sponsorship language could become marketing without increasing real access.

**Disconfirming signal:** Participants report lower service quality, reduced choice, or pressure to serve employer branding goals.

### Frontline operator

**Thesis:** The scarce resource is not curriculum; it is reliable staff time for selling, adapting, delivering, invoicing, and supporting customers.

**Strongest proposal:** Use one standardized curriculum, one delivery day per month, a named pilot owner, and a maximum of three cohorts before review.

**Risk:** Even a small pilot can create hidden coordination work across all six staff.

**Disconfirming signal:** More than twenty percent of pilot effort falls outside planned delivery and preparation time.

## Deliberation ledger excerpt

| Claim | Type | Status | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employers will pay | ASSUMPTION | unverified | No service exists without demand |
| Current staff have spare capacity | ASSUMPTION | contested | Determines mission and burnout risk |
| Standardization improves economics | INFERENCE | provisional | Shapes service design |
| Free access remains non-negotiable | VALUE and user constraint | supported for this brief | Sets the mission floor |
| Sponsorship increases beneficiary value | INFERENCE | contested | Could justify the paid offer or become symbolic |

## Targeted cross-examination

### Operator critiques the constructive explorer

**Steelman:** A standardized pilot is more reversible and less distracting than building a consulting practice.

**Material objection:** The proposal treats standardization as a capacity solution but ignores selling, contracting, and customer support.

**Unsupported assumption:** One delivery day represents most of the workload.

**Repair:** Cap total pilot hours, record work by activity, and appoint one owner with authority to reject customization.

### Beneficiary advocate critiques the sponsorship mechanism

**Steelman:** Connecting employer revenue to free access makes the mission benefit visible.

**Material objection:** Counting “seats funded” can reward volume while participant quality falls.

**Unsupported assumption:** More seats equal more benefit.

**Repair:** Track attendance, completion, participant-reported value, and coaching access—not only nominal places.

### Auditor critiques the apparent preference for a pilot

**Steelman:** A bounded pilot is reversible and information-rich.

**Material objection:** A free or heavily discounted pilot would not test willingness to pay or unit economics.

**Unsupported assumption:** Positive feedback predicts purchase behavior.

**Repair:** Charge a real price, require a purchase decision by a budget holder, and calculate full cost including sales and administration.

## Revisions

The constructive explorer accepts the operator's critique and revises the pilot to include a total-hours ceiling, fixed scope, and no custom modules. It accepts the auditor's critique and requires a paid contract rather than a free proof of concept.

The beneficiary advocate accepts that “seats funded” is insufficient and revises the benefit rule to use participant outcomes and service-quality guardrails.

The challenger retains its governance condition but agrees it can be satisfied through a short board-approved pilot charter rather than a permanent policy before learning begins.

# Think Tank Report

**Mode:** Standard  
**Independence:** Full — five isolated first-round subagents were used before peer proposals were shared; model and training-data biases may still be correlated.  
**Question:** Which viable models could add dependable earned income without weakening free community access or staff capacity?

## Problem framing

The nonprofit needs more dependable income, but the binding constraint may be staff attention rather than curriculum. [FACT] Free workshops must continue and the current team has six full-time staff. [ASSUMPTION] Employers will pay for the nonprofit's expertise. [UNKNOWN] Demand, full delivery cost, staff capacity, and repeat purchase intent remain untested. The useful solution space therefore includes not only different products, but different relationships among payer, beneficiary, and delivery capacity.

## Council composition

- Constructive explorer — developed distinct earned-income mechanisms.
- Critical challenger — tested mission drift and operational failure.
- Evidence and assumptions auditor — classified claims and validation needs.
- Beneficiary advocate — protected access and participant outcomes.
- Frontline operator — tested staffing, workflow, and overload risk.

## Perspective briefs

### Perspective: Constructive explorer

The strongest opportunity is a standardized employer cohort rather than bespoke consulting, combined with an explicit community benefit. It would change position if employers require extensive customization or show low repeat intent.

### Perspective: Critical challenger

Revenue should not receive priority by default. A pilot needs a mission floor, capacity ceiling, full-cost accounting, and a stop condition. The critique softened from “governance before launch” to a lightweight board-approved pilot charter.

### Perspective: Evidence and assumptions auditor

Willingness to pay, available staff capacity, and positive unit economics are unverified. A real paid contract is necessary; free pilot enthusiasm would not resolve the core uncertainty.

### Perspective: Beneficiary advocate

The paid service should create measurable beneficiary value without allowing employer branding or scheduling needs to degrade free services. Nominal “seats funded” are insufficient without quality and outcome measures.

### Perspective: Frontline operator

Selling, contracting, preparation, invoicing, support, and recovery time are likely to dominate hidden workload. Fixed scope, a named owner, a total-hours ceiling, and no customization are essential to a valid pilot.

## Areas of convergence

All perspectives support a bounded experiment rather than an immediate service-line build. They converge on fixed scope, real pricing, full-cost measurement, protection of free-program quality, and an explicit review point. This convergence does not verify employer demand or spare capacity.

## Material disagreements

The main tension is whether community sponsorship should be a defining component or a later use of surplus. The beneficiary advocate sees it as part of legitimacy; the operator worries that it adds complexity before unit economics are known. A second tension is whether existing staff can own the pilot or whether temporary capacity is required. These questions change the design and should be tested, not settled by vote.

## Contested assumptions

- [ASSUMPTION] Employers will pay a price that covers full delivery and acquisition cost.
- [ASSUMPTION] A fixed offer can meet buyer needs without customization.
- [ASSUMPTION] One staff owner can run the pilot within a bounded hours budget.
- [INFERENCE] Linking revenue to participant benefit will protect mission focus.
- [VALUE] Free community access has priority over maximizing earned income.
- [UNKNOWN] Whether sponsors value the community-benefit component enough to improve purchase or renewal.

## Solution sets

### Solution set 1: Paid standardized pilot

**Optimizes for:** Fast learning about demand, cost, and capacity.

**Core components:** One fixed curriculum, one cohort, real price, three-customer maximum, named owner, total-hours ceiling, and full-cost accounting.

**Deliberately excludes:** Custom modules, broad marketing, permanent hires, and promised scale.

**Assumptions and dependencies:** A useful employer problem can be solved with a standard offer and the board can approve a short pilot charter.

**Trade-offs and risk owners:** The nonprofit gains high-quality evidence but staff bear workload risk; employers receive less customization.

**Safeguards and mitigations:** Free-program service floor, stop condition, workload log, and no launch dates that conflict with community delivery.

**Early test or signal:** Three purchase decisions from budget holders and measured gross contribution after all hours.

**Choose this when:** The primary goal is to learn whether a viable service exists before adding mission mechanisms.

### Solution set 2: Employer-funded community cohort

**Optimizes for:** Direct mission alignment and a clear beneficiary story.

**Core components:** Employers buy training and fund a paired community workshop or coaching allocation measured by participant outcomes.

**Deliberately excludes:** Revenue uses with no visible community link and seat-count-only impact claims.

**Assumptions and dependencies:** Employers value the paired benefit and the nonprofit can measure participant quality without excessive administration.

**Trade-offs and risk owners:** The mission link is stronger, but operations and impact measurement are more complex. Beneficiaries risk becoming part of employer marketing.

**Safeguards and mitigations:** Participant consent, outcome measures, limits on sponsor influence, and independent scheduling of free services.

**Early test or signal:** Buyer preference between a plain paid cohort and a paired-benefit offer at transparent prices.

**Choose this when:** Employer buyers value social impact and the added complexity does not consume the contribution margin.

### Solution set 3: Licensed curriculum partnership

**Optimizes for:** Income with low direct delivery load.

**Core components:** Package the curriculum, train delivery partners, license use, and audit quality.

**Deliberately excludes:** Direct employer delivery by the nonprofit and rapid rollout before curriculum codification.

**Assumptions and dependencies:** The curriculum is transferable, partners can deliver credibly, and intellectual-property and quality terms are manageable.

**Trade-offs and risk owners:** Staff delivery load falls, but control and near-term revenue may be lower. Partners bear delivery risk; the nonprofit bears reputation risk.

**Safeguards and mitigations:** Limited license, certification, observation, participant feedback, and termination rights.

**Early test or signal:** One qualified partner can deliver a supervised cohort at acceptable quality with less nonprofit staff time.

**Choose this when:** Capacity is the binding constraint and the curriculum is sufficiently mature to transfer.

## Stress test

Pre-mortem: twelve months after launch, earned income is small and free-program quality has fallen. Likely causes are hidden sales/support time, customization creep, sponsor influence, and optimistic accounting. Earliest signals are workload above the ceiling, missed free-program preparation, repeated exceptions to scope, and contribution margin that disappears after staff hours. The stress test strengthens the fixed-scope and full-cost conditions and makes a three-customer review gate mandatory.

## Current synthesis

The strongest current direction is Solution set 1: a real-priced, fixed-scope pilot with a lightweight mission and capacity charter. It provides the cleanest evidence on the two leading uncertainties—willingness to pay and true staff cost—without committing to a permanent service model. It deliberately excludes customization and scale. The paired community-benefit mechanism should be tested as a buyer option during discovery rather than assumed from the start.

## Minority report

The beneficiary advocate favors Solution set 2 from the first pilot because mission alignment should be designed in, not added after commercial incentives form. This view becomes stronger if employer interviews show that the paired benefit materially increases willingness to pay or if the board believes any unlinked earned-income offer would create unacceptable mission risk.

## Unresolved uncertainties

The nonprofit lacks evidence on buyer authority, price tolerance, full workload, repeat demand, staff interest, and whether the social-impact component changes buying behavior. It also needs a baseline measure of free-program quality so pilot effects can be detected.

## Next validation step

Run ten structured interviews with employer budget holders, present two priced concepts—plain standardized training and paired community benefit—and seek three conditional purchase commitments. In parallel, time-box an internal delivery simulation and calculate full cost. Use the results to decide whether to run one paid cohort, redesign the offer, or stop.
