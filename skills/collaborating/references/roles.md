# Role Library and Orthogonality

Roles are mandates, not personalities. “Optimist,” “pessimist,” and “expert” are weak because they do not define what evidence, objective, stakeholder, or failure class the role owns.

## Mandatory functions

### Constructive explorer

**Mandate:** Expand the possibility space and develop viable mechanisms without assuming the obvious options are exhaustive.

**Primary questions:** What different ways could the outcome be achieved? Which combinations create new options? What reversible experiments reveal more?

**Boundary:** Do not spend the response merely criticizing existing ideas.

### Critical challenger

**Mandate:** Find the most consequential failure modes, contradictions, and neglected trade-offs, then propose repairs.

**Primary questions:** What breaks first? Which stakeholder or dependency is being ignored? Which claim is doing too much work?

**Boundary:** Do not reject ambition merely because execution is difficult; distinguish fatal flaws from repairable ones.

### Evidence and assumptions auditor

**Mandate:** Classify claims, assess evidence quality, identify factual disputes, and state what would resolve them.

**Primary questions:** What is known? What is assumed? What is inferred? Which values are being presented as facts? What evidence is missing?

**Boundary:** Do not choose a solution solely because it has more cited facts; relevance and causal fit still matter.

## Adaptive roles

Choose roles that cover the largest gaps in the Shared Brief.

### Domain practitioner

Tests whether proposals match real operating conditions, tacit constraints, and professional practice. Best when abstract strategy may fail in the field.

### User or beneficiary advocate

Represents actual user behavior, accessibility, adoption friction, journey continuity, and who receives value. Best when solution designers may optimize internal convenience.

### Frontline operator

Examines workflows, staffing, handoffs, incentives, incident recovery, and maintenance burden. Best when a proposal must survive daily execution.

### Systems thinker

Maps feedback loops, dependencies, second-order effects, path dependence, and system boundaries. Best when local improvements could create global harm.

### Economist or incentives analyst

Examines costs, opportunity costs, incentives, market responses, pricing, and resource allocation. Best when behavior depends on rewards or scarce resources.

### Governance and decision-rights analyst

Clarifies authority, accountability, escalation, reversibility, and conflict resolution. Best for organizational, platform, partnership, or policy designs.

### Safety, ethics, and misuse reviewer

Examines foreseeable harm, abuse cases, unequal impact, vulnerable groups, and safeguards. Best when the proposal changes power, access, privacy, or safety.

### Simplicity and reversibility advocate

Finds the smallest useful intervention, removes unnecessary machinery, and prefers experiments that preserve optionality. Best when scope or institutional lock-in is a risk.

### Long-horizon steward

Examines durability, environmental or institutional externalities, technical debt, capability erosion, and future option value. Best when short-term success can create long-term fragility.

### Contrarian alternative builder

Develops the strongest coherent approach outside the apparent consensus. Best when anchoring or institutional preference is likely. This role must produce a viable alternative, not reflexive opposition.

### Integration architect

Looks for interfaces among otherwise competing proposals and identifies which components can coexist without blurring their governing logic. Best when the space contains modular mechanisms.

### Measurement and experimentation designer

Turns contested assumptions into measurable hypotheses, experiments, leading indicators, and stop/scale criteria. Best when evidence can be gathered before commitment.

### Equity and distribution analyst

Examines how benefits, costs, risk, voice, and access are distributed across groups. Best when aggregate outcomes could conceal unequal burdens.

### Adversarial environment analyst

Assumes strategic opponents, competitors, fraud, gaming, or regulatory response. Best when other actors can adapt against the proposal.

## Selection algorithm

1. Include the three mandatory functions.
2. Identify missing dimensions across:
   - stakeholder;
   - objective;
   - system layer;
   - time horizon;
   - evidence type;
   - failure class.
3. Add the role that covers the largest decision-relevant gap.
4. Repeat until the mode's council size is reached.
5. Run the orthogonality test below.

## Orthogonality test

For each pair of roles, compare four fields:

| Field | Question |
|---|---|
| Objective | What outcome is this role trying to protect or improve? |
| Lens | Which stakeholder, system layer, or time horizon does it own? |
| Evidence | What kind of evidence or experience does it privilege? |
| Failure class | What can this role detect that others are likely to miss? |

If two roles share three or four fields, they are redundant. Merge their useful constraints and replace one.

### Weak council

- Business strategist
- Commercial strategist
- Growth strategist
- Corporate strategist
- Product strategist

These names differ, but their objective and evidence lens probably overlap.

### Stronger replacement

- Constructive explorer
- Critical challenger
- Evidence and assumptions auditor
- Frontline operator
- User advocate

The stronger council covers possibility creation, failure analysis, epistemic status, execution, and adoption.

## Role prompt checklist

A role instruction is complete when it states:

- the unique mandate;
- the primary questions;
- what evidence or stakeholder lens it owns;
- what it must not duplicate;
- the common response contract;
- the Shared Brief;
- the evidence trust boundary.
